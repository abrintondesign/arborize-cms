When You Need Your Trees Pruned

Most trees in residential yards across Simcoe County don't get pruned often enough — and when they do, it's often done wrong. Here's when it makes sense to call:

  • Branches are overhanging the roof, a fence, or a neighbouring property
  • Dead or broken limbs are a falling hazard
  • The tree hasn't been touched in years and is getting dense
  • You've had storm damage and need a cleanup and structural assessment
  • You want to improve light or clearance without removing the tree
  • A young tree needs early training to develop good structure

What's Included

  • Full assessment of the tree before any cuts are made
  • Crown cleaning — removing dead, diseased, or crossing branches
  • Crown thinning to improve light and air movement
  • Hazard pruning — removing limbs that pose a risk to structures or people
  • Clearance pruning for buildings, utility lines, and driveways
  • Structural pruning on young trees to establish good form
  • All brush and debris removed or chipped from the property

How I Approach Pruning

Before I make a single cut, I look at the whole tree. What's the species? What's its growth habit? Where is it in its life cycle? What's the goal — safety, aesthetics, clearance, health?

ISA standards mean I make cuts at the right location — at the branch collar, not flush with the trunk and not leaving long stubs. Both of those common mistakes create entry points for decay and can compromise the tree for years.

I also won't remove more than about 25% of the live canopy in a single season. Removing too much at once stresses the tree and triggers excessive water sprout growth. I'll tell you if the work needs to be phased over a couple of seasons.

Why I Don't Top Trees

Topping — cutting the main stems back to stubs — is one of the most damaging things you can do to a tree. It creates massive wounds the tree can't properly close, leads to weak water sprout regrowth that breaks easily, and often just delays the need for removal by a few years while causing stress in the meantime.

If someone quoted you tree topping as a solution to a tall tree or clearance problem, there's almost always a better way. I'll show you what that looks like.
